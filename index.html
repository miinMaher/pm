Chapter 2

Initiation Phase

A comprehensive guide to project analysis, selection, and justification.

"Should we actually do this project?"

1. Project Initiation & Main Outputs

Deciding whether to proceed involves analyzing four key pillars:

Project Mission

The "Why" - The core reason for the project's existence.

Project Objectives

The "What" - Specific achievements we are aiming for.

Project Deliverables

The "Steps" - Tangible items needed to reach objectives.

Project Stakeholders

The "Who" - Anyone with an interest in the results.

2. Project Complexity

Project Requirements

  • S
    Stable: Requirements known and remain steady.
  • E
    Evolving: High probability of change; developing something new.

Challenge to Capability

  • L
    Low: In-house skills, staff, and systems are sufficient.
  • H
    High: External help needed (lack of skills or systems).
Complexity is a matrix of these two variables, affecting Structure, Staffing, and Culture.

3. Project Scope & Scope Creep

The Scope Document

Determines work boundaries. Must define:

Deliverables Constraints Assumptions Exclusions (OUT OF SCOPE)

Scope Creep ⚠️

"The incremental expansion of project scope without documented changes."

  • Threatens project success
  • Requires un-planned resources
  • Creates confusion and budget overruns

4. Project Constraints

Every project is limited by these five intersecting factors:

COST
TIME
RESOURCES
SCOPE
QUALITY
Quality is the common understanding of what is being delivered.

5. Project Selection Process

1

Call for Projects

Committee requests goals, deliverables, business cases, and cost-benefit analyses.

2

Review Meeting

Assessment based on market share, strategic vision, and public perception.

3

Prioritization

"Poego" projects are weeded out; the rest are ranked by importance and benefit.

4

Reporting & Reprioritization

Active projects are monitored; new projects can be added mid-year.

6. Business Justification

The Business Case

A justification for the proposed project. Includes:

  • Cost-benefit analysis
  • Market demand context
  • Regulatory requirements
  • Technological advances

SMART Evaluation

Specific: Real business problem?

Measurable: Baseline and targets?

Attainable: Realistic goal?

Relevant: Business objective?

Time Bound: Set completion date?

7. Evaluation Method: Cost-Benefit

Sound Project = Costs < Benefits

Cost Factors:

  • • Product development (Staff/Hardware)
  • • Marketing & Sales (Ads/Demos)
  • • Ongoing Operational Support

The Bank Software Example:

Total Cost: $5 Million

Projected Revenue: $12 Million

Decision: RECOMMENDED (GO)

Agreements (Contracts, MoUs, Emails) formalize these evaluations into legal commitments.
